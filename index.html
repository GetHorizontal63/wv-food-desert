Starving Out the Workers: Food Desertification of the Southern WV Coal Fields

In 1960, John F. Kennedy campaigned through the coal fields of Appalachia. He came asking for votes and found something he hadn't expected to find just a day's drive from Washington: open, undisguised hunger. He won West Virginia. He won the presidency. And when his administration moved to build a federal food assistance program, the faces he'd seen in McDowell County were part of what drove it. The modern SNAP program traces a direct line back to what a United States President witnessed on the streets of a small West Virginia community.

Sixty-five years later, the SNAP program still exists but the stores meant to feed participants do not. The collapse of coal didn't produce a managed transition, it produced a chaotic landscape of fast-food chains, dollar stores, and gas station vegetables. What's left is a region less than four hours from the White House where one in five households can't reliably feed itself while the rest struggle to maintain any semblance of a healthy diet.

The Region

This is not a story about poverty as an abstraction. After all, these counties were not always poor. At the peak of the coal era, McDowell County alone was home to nearly 100,000 people (the third most populous county in the state at the time) with a Median Household Income of $2,527 per year, just 15.5% below the national level. Today, the gap is 53.3%. The county boasted bustling department stores, vibrant movie theaters, a strong hospital system, and an active tax base. The extraction economy made those things possible and then, as the seams ran thin and the politics changed, it unmade them. What was left were people who couldn't find steady work as the country moved on, letting critical infrastructure crumble.

The failure of the United States to provide for the communities that fueled titanic growth and world changing war efforts is most evident in the local food supply. Towns can absorb the loss of a department store or drive a little further for the new blockbuster, but they cannot feed themselves without the fresh produce of a local grocery store.

When the Stores Closed

On the map to the right, every dot represents a SNAP-authorized grocery retailer in the 19-county study area, drawn from USDA-FNS's Historical SNAP Retailer Locator files. As the map advances from 2005 to 2025, red pulses mark store closures, green pulses mark new authorizations, and amber pulses mark replacements: a closure and a new authorization at the same parcel within the same five-year window.

Dot size reflects store category: the larger the circle, the more comprehensive the grocer's offerings:

  • Supermarket / Super Store. Full perishable lines including meat, dairy, produce, and bread, plus extensive non-food. Annual sales are typically over $2M. This is the store most Americans think of when they run to grab a gallon of milk. (Kroger, Walmart Supercenter, Publix, Safeway, Giant, Food Lion, Harris Teeter, Meijer, H-E-B)
  • Large / Medium / Small Grocery. All four staple categories with at least some perishables; smaller floor space and narrower assortment as you go down. (ALDI, Trader Joe's, Save-A-Lot, IGA, Weis Markets, Grocery Outlet, WinCo Foods)
  • Combination Grocery / Other. Convenience stores, dollar stores, gas stations, and pharmacies that meet the federal minimum (36 staple-food units across the four categories) but where groceries are a sideline, not the main business. (Dollar General, Family Dollar, Dollar Tree, CVS, Walgreens, 7-Eleven, Casey's, Circle K)

From 2005 to 2025, the stores that can actually feed a family - supermarkets and full-service grocers - fell from 224 to 159. A 29% collapse. Large grocery stores in rural localities, the kind that anchored small-town main streets for decades, fell from 32 to 12. Sixty-three percent. Gone in twenty years.

The headline total tells a different story: the count of available SNAP-authorized retailers climbed from 604 to 987, a 63% increase. That number is what gets reported and what Congress sees. It is a lie of categorization; after 2008, the USDA expanded SNAP authorization to convenience stores, dollar stores, and gas stations, and those locations now dominate the count. Strip them out and the collapse is unmistakable.

Who's Out of Reach

Whitesville, WV, a small town of a few hundred people, straddles the border of Raleigh and Boone counties. For decades, residents shopped at half a dozen grocery stores and supermarkets throughout the town. Families from neighboring communities would come downtown to visit, shop, and stock their fridge for the week. In 2015, 6 grocery stores and a dozen convenience stores served these communities. As of December 2025, the town is home to a single Dollar General and a pair of gas stations selling canned beans.

As stores close and supermarkets move away from thousands of families, the cost associated with accessing fresh produce and reliable sources of meat are exacerbated by tremendous travel times. In March 2015, the average resident of Whitesville traveled less than 2 miles round trip to buy fresh apples. Today, those same fruits are over 25 miles away.

To the right, we have mapped all Supermarket and Super Store locations (open and closed) from 2005 to 2025. Plotted against them is every residential use building in the 19 county survey region. (Microsoft USBuildingFootprints joined to OpenStreetMap use-tags, roughly 382,000 structures)

Each home is colored by the change in driving distance to its nearest full-service grocer between 2005 and 2025, measured along the OSM road network, to determine changes in accessibility over time. And the road network is key, here, as West Virginians must contend with terrain seen almost nowhere else in the country. While the nearest supermarket to Whitesville is a mere 17 miles as the crow flies, the switchbacks of Appalachia extend a 30 minute grocery run into a 50 mile, hour long trek through the mountains. The same topography that made coal extraction possible - that built these communities - is the same topography that makes food access a fundamentally different problem here than it is in a flat exurb with a Walmart every eight miles.

On our map, homes marked red saw the road travel distance to the nearest SNAP eligible grocery or convenience store grow from 2005 to 2025. Homes shaded green saw a new store open nearby. The deeper the red, the greater the loss.

The Homes Left Behind

Here is the same map, stripped to the consequence. We are looking at every home which saw road travel distance to the nearest Supermarket or Super Store grow by at least half a mile between 2005 and 2025. All 87,702 of them. Active stores have been filtered, leaving orange rings where full-service grocers once stood.

The card counts those homes and reports the median miles added to their nearest-grocer trip, not as a statistic, but as a weekly tax on the time and fuel of the households least able to pay it. The arithmetic is straightforward: a median loss of over a mile each way, run twice a week, fifty-two weeks a year, in a region where vehicle reliability is not a given and gas is not cheap. That is not an inconvenience. That is a structural barrier to nutrition that compounds silently, every week, every year.

Nobody announced this. There was no policy decision, no vote, no public deliberation about whether these communities should lose access to fresh food. A chain ran the numbers. The store closed. The valley went quiet.

5

The Desert, Drawn

The USDA's Food Access Research Atlas, the dataset Congress uses to allocate Healthy Food Financing Initiative dollars, calls a place a food desert when residents have to travel an unreasonable distance to reach a full-service grocer. The thresholds were calibrated against the Economic Research Service's National Household Food Acquisition Survey which found that half a mile is roughly the limit a typical urban shopper will walk with groceries while 10 miles is the round-trip distance at which rural households start substituting convenience stores and dollar stores for full grocery runs. Past those cutoffs, behavior changes: less fresh produce, more shelf-stable calories, worse health outcomes. The research is consistent and decades old but the policy response has not matched reality on the ground.

Unfortunately, the USDA Food Access Research Atlas definition of a Food Desert struggles in mountainous terrain. The Atlas uses straight-line distance between grid cell centroids, not road network distance. Specifically, the Atlas measures the distance, "between the geographic center of the half-kilometer square grid that contains estimates of the population and the center of the grid with the nearest supermarket." In other words, it measures how far a bird flies, not how far a person drives. No road network. No terrain. No consideration for the people treated as numbers.

For flat exurban America, that approximation is probably close enough. For southern WV it is systematically wrong in a way that consistently undercounts the access problem. A household that sits 8 miles as the crow flies from a supermarket might be 25 miles by gravel road through abandoned coal fields. That household, per the USDA definition, does not qualify as a food desert and, thus, would not receive the same consideration for priority funding from Congress.

Every parcel more than half a mile by road from a Supermarket or Super Store inside a Census Urban Area (Charleston, Beckley, Bluefield, Huntington fringe), or more than 10 miles by road everywhere else, is shaded. Blue is desert that disappeared by 2025; red is desert that grew; purple is land that has been a desert across both decades. The squares in the legend are drawn to map scale: that's the actual footprint of each change, sitting on the same ridges, hollers, and valley corridors below.

Most of what you're looking at is purple. Not red, not desert that grew after 2005. Purple. Land that was already a food desert twenty years ago and still is today. The closures since 2005 made things worse at the margins. The structural abandonment predates all of it, predates the study window, predates the methodology used to measure it. What the red shows you is a worsening crisis. What the purple shows you is a crisis that was never addressed in the first place.

6

The SNAP Paradox

When Kennedy traveled Appalachia, he saw hunger. And he followed through on his promise to the workers of West Virginia. But today, the pitfalls are laid bare. While the SNAP program now reaches one in five households in this region, the stores that are meant to feed Americans left behind by the rest of the country are few and far between.

On this map, each Census tract is shaded by the share of households receiving SNAP benefits in the last twelve months of the 2018-2022 American Community Survey 5-Year Estimates Table B22003. In our survey area, 52,410 households were on SNAP, roughly one in five (20.9%). The statewide rate for West Virginia sits at 16.6% while the national average hovers around 12.6%. One tract in particular, McDowell 9538, sees 54.1% of households depend on SNAP benefits. Tracts in Mingo and Logan are not far behind with half of all households relying on the benefit. County-aggregated leaders, McDowell (37.3%), Mingo (34.3%), Webster (28.8%), Clay (28.7%), and Logan (27.7%), regularly rank in the 100 most SNAP-dependent counties of the United States.

Also plotted here are all SNAP-authorized full-service grocers (Supermarket, Super Store, or Large/Medium/Small Grocery) operating in 2025, drawn from the same USDA-FNS Historical SNAP Retailer Locator file used in the maps above. Again, larger dots are Supermarkets and Super Stores; smaller dots are Large/Medium/Small grocers.

Now compare the two layers. When we look at SNAP-authorized stores, the pattern inverts. Clay County, with the fourth-highest SNAP rate in West Virginia, has zero supermarkets. Mingo has one. McDowell has three SNAP-authorized supermarkets across 535 square miles. Kanawha County, the urban anchor with the lowest SNAP rate in the study area (15.5%), holds 27 of the region's 85 supermarkets.

Pearson correlation at the county level between SNAP enrollment rate and supermarkets per 10,000 SNAP households is r = -0.56, confirming what we see: the places where the most households need SNAP are the same places where the program's intended retail channel is thinnest. The benefit is delivered but the store it was meant to be spent in is forty-five minutes away.

The program works, technically. The transfer happens. The debit card loads on schedule. What the program cannot do is conjure a grocery store into a county that the market has decided is not worth serving. What's left in town is the gas station and the dollar store: SNAP-authorized, but stocked with shelf-stable calories, not groceries.

Kennedy came here because he needed votes. What he found convinced him that the federal government had an obligation to act. Sixty-five years later, the government is still acting, still sending the benefit, still meeting its technical obligation. But the question his visit raised, whether a person living in these hollows could actually feed their family, remains unanswered. Not because the question is hard, but because no one with the power to answer it has chosen to.
